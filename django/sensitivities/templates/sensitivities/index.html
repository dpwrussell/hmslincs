A dose-response curve is the most fundamental measure of cellular response to perturbing agents and drugs. The curve measures the magnitude of an effect (in this description, cell viability) across a range of drug concentrations and is typically illustrated in a log-linear plot. The most common parameter derived from this relationship is IC50, the point at which half-maximal effect is achieved (this is often referred to as the “potency” of a perturbagen). However, variation in maximum effect at the highest doses (Emax) is also common. Capturing these sources of variation requires a multi-parametric description involving variables such as IC50, Emax and the slope of the curve. where D is drug concentration, E(D) is a measured response parameter, E0 and E∞ are the top and bottom asymptotes of the response curve, EC50 is the concentration at half-maximal effect (hme), and HS is a slope parameter analogous to a Hill coefficient.

 

Three measures of drug response derived from this equation are in common use: (i) IC50, the concentration of drug at which response is half its theoretical maximum (i.e. E(D)/E0 = 0.5); (ii) the value of the effect at the maximum concentration of drug tested (Emax = E(Dmax)); and (iii) the area under the dose-response curve (AUC) also known as activity area.

 

Figure 1. A canonical dose-response curve with parameters from equation 1 illustrated.

If we examine variation in the responses of ~50 breast cancer cell lines to the drug fascaplysin, a natural product and selective inhibitor of the cyclin dependent kinase 4, as described by Heiser et al. (2012), we see a classic situation in which IC50 varies, Emax = 0 (i.e complete killing at the highest dose) and the Hill slope parameter (HS) is approximately 1 (Figure 2; red curves). However, if we look at the variation in responses to docetaxel we observe variation both in Emax and HS (Figure 2; blue curves). We have recently performed a systematic analysis of large-scale dose-response data arising from LINCS experiments and published cell line encyclopedias, and a manuscript describing this work is currently in review. All analytical tools, raw data and other supplementary materials will be posted on this site soon.
